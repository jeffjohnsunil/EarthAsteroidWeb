What do you mean?

Surrounding the Earth, there exists a large group of asteroids that orbit the Earth... very closely. Some of these are made of rocks, but the truth is, most of them are man-made, or more specifically, the waste that rockets dispose of when they exit the atmosphere of the Earth. As of November 4, 2021, 130 million pieces of debris larger than 1 millimeter in orbit around the Earth, 2,224 of which are both sufficiently large and come sufficiently close to Earth to be considered potentially hazardous, and these asteroids will last for millions of years.



Orbital Periods

These asteroids can take centuries to orbit depending on the height of orbit. Others will fall back to Earth within several years. Some are in between and just float around.

Orbital Speeds

They travel at speeds up to 17,500 mph, fast enough for a relatively small piece of orbital debris to damage a satellite or a spacecraft. here are approximately 100 million pieces of debris about .04 inches (or one millimeter) and larger.

Tracking Debris

The Department of Defense and NASA maintains a highly accurate satellite catalog on objects in Earth orbit. Most of the cataloged objects are larger than a softball (approximately 10 centimeters).



Facts and Figures

The amount of debris that orbits the Earth has been increasing exponentially since the first space debris was discovered in 1959. Also, a lot of this debris isn't even functional spacecraft - it's mainly the parts that rockets dispatch and excess rocket fuel. Learn more by navigating to the Info page. Note that the above stats are only estimates.



Which ones are the threats?

Any asteroid that has a diameter approximately 35 meters across can pose major threats to an area, but any object larger than a millimeter in diameter would be enough to kill someone.



How are they classified as being near the Earth?

The technical truth is that any Near Earth Object should have a trajectory that brings it to within approximately 45 million kilometers of the Earth's orbit.
